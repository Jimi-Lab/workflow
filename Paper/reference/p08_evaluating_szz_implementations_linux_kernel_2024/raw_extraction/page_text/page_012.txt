To investigate the impact of ghost commits on SZZ algo-
rithms, we remove the Remove and Add Mapping Ghost cases
from KFC dataset. The experimental results for this modiﬁed
dataset are presented in Table VI, which should be compared
to the original results in Table II. “Filter RMG” refers to the re-
moval of Remove Mapping Ghost bug-ﬁxing commits from our
dataset. “Filter AMG” indicates the removal of Add Mapping
Ghost bug-inducing commits from our dataset. “Filter RMG,
AMG” signiﬁes the removal of both types of commits from
our dataset. For example, when considering the B-SZZ algo-
rithm, as shown in Table VI, only ﬁltering Add Mapping Ghost
reduces the recall from 0.71 to 0.58 (↓22.4%), compared to
ﬁltering both Remove and Add Mapping Ghost. While ﬁltering
only Remove Mapping Ghost leads to a decrease in recall to 0.7
(↓1.4%). These ﬁndings suggest that Remove Mapping Ghost
has a more pronounced negative impact on the recall of SZZ al-
gorithms. When compared to the results in Table II, the average
recall for all SZZ algorithms in the KFC dataset improves by
22.2% after the removal of Remove and Add Mapping Ghost.
The precision of all SZZ algorithms remains unchanged due
to the SZZ algorithms not producing any results for Remove
Mapping Ghost cases and the relatively small proportion of Add
Mapping Ghost cases.

We eliminate bug-ﬁxing commits classiﬁed as Success, Partial
Success, and Remove Mapping Ghost from the KFC dataset,
then search for Add Mapping Ghost cases among the remaining
bug-ﬁxing commits. If all bug-inducing commits for a speciﬁc
bug-ﬁxing commit are Add Mapping Ghost, we consider that
B-SZZ’s failure is due to Add Mapping Ghost.
Out of 584 bug-ﬁxing commits where at least one of their
bug-inducing commits is Add Mapping Ghost, 361 fail as a
result of Add Mapping Ghost. Upon completing these ﬁlters, we
obtain a dataset consisting of 17,552 bug-ﬁxing commits where
B-SZZ fails, which we refer to as “Failures without MG.”
With our algorithm, using TC-SZZ, we ﬁnd that out of the
17,552 bug-ﬁxing commits in “Failure without MG,” 3,115
(17.7%) of those commits contain 3,163 bug-inducing commits
within the change history of some removed line. In contrast,
git log -L only manages to track down 2,800. We refer to
the failures that can be solved by TC-SZZ as Line Change
Detections.
For these 3,115 bug-ﬁxing commits, the average length of the
change history is 3.76, with the longest change history having
length 35. Additionally, 1,272 bug-inducing commits occupy
the second-closest position in the change history (with a total
length ≥2), signifying that these bug-inducing commits can
be located by using git blame twice. Furthermore, 1,489 bug-
inducing commits are located at the furthest point in the change
history, indicating that the initial code is faulty. The remaining
354 commits are in intermediate positions, i.e. descendant com-
mits in Fig. 1.
