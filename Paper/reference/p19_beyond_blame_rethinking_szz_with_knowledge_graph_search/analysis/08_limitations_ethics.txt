Stated Limitations:
- While our evaluation did not directly measure the relationship between blame count and accuracy, this pattern suggests that blame complexity could serve as an indicator of case difficulty, warranting future investigation.
- Simple cases may benefit from fast-path heuristics.Projects with predominantly single- blame cases (e.g., DS_LINUX where 47% have 1 to 2 deleted lines with 1 blame commit) may benefit from fast-path optimizations that resolve cases with minimal LLM interaction.
- Future work should systematically evaluate accuracy stratified by blame complexity to confirm this hypothesis.
- For contexts where BIC identification directly impacts downstream tasks such as defect prediction model training or automated program repair, this cost may be justified by the improved accuracy.
- While SZZ operates on an implicit assumption that bug-inducing and bug-fixing commits modify the same code elements [42], this assumption does not always hold: prior work has shown that over 13% of BICs share no files with their corresponding bug-fixing commits, and 17% of bug fixes contain only added lines where blame cannot be applied [23].
- Despite these fundamental limitations, prior work has focused primarily on improving precision of the git blame-based approach through filtering heuristics [7, 19], refactoring detection [24, 25], deep learning [35], semantic analysis [4, 37], and LLM-based reasoning [36], leaving the recall problem unaddressed.
